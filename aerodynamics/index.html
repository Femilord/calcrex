Lift & Drag — The Two Fundamental Aerodynamic Forces

How air flowing over a body creates forces perpendicular (lift) and parallel (drag) to the flow direction.

1 The Lift Equation

L = ½ρv²SC_L
ρ = air density (kg/m³), v = airspeed (m/s)S = wing planform area (m²)C_L = lift coefficient (depends on shape & angle of attack)
Airfoil cross-section: The curved upper surface forces air to travel faster (lower pressure), while slower flow beneath maintains higher pressure. The pressure difference creates an upward lift force. The angle of attack (α) amplifies this effect.

2 The Drag Equation

D = ½ρv²SC_D
C_D = drag coefficientTotal drag = parasite drag + induced dragInduced drag ∝ 1/v² (decreases with speed)
Sphere
C_D ≈ 0.47
Flat plate (⊥)
C_D ≈ 1.28
Streamlined body
C_D ≈ 0.04
Car
C_D ≈ 0.25–0.35
Cyclist
C_D ≈ 0.88
Clean aircraft wing
C_D ≈ 0.02–0.04
Worked Example 1
Calculating Lift for Level Flight
Problem: A Cessna 172 weighs 10,900 N, has a wing area of 16.2 m², and flies at 55 m/s at sea level (ρ = 1.225 kg/m³). What C_L is required for level flight?
Level flight: L = W
10900 = ½ × 1.225 × 55² × 16.2 × C_L
10900 = ½ × 1.225 × 3025 × 16.2 × C_L
10900 = 30,024 × C_L
C_L = 0.363
Answer: C_L = 0.363. A typical airfoil can produce C_L up to ~1.6, so 0.363 is comfortable cruise — plenty of margin before stall.

Worked Example 2
Drag Force and Power at Highway Speed
Problem: A car has frontal area 2.2 m², C_D = 0.30, at 120 km/h (33.3 m/s). What is the aerodynamic drag force and power to overcome it?
Drag and power
D = ½ × 1.225 × 33.3² × 2.2 × 0.30
= ½ × 1.225 × 1108.9 × 0.66
D = 448.5 N
P = D × v = 448.5 × 33.3
P = 14,935 W ≈ 20 hp
Answer: 449 N drag, 20 hp to overcome it. At 160 km/h, drag nearly doubles to ~800 N and power triples to ~47 hp. Drag ∝ v² but power ∝ v³ — speed is expensive.

Worked Example 3
Lift-to-Drag Ratio
Problem: A glider has L/D = 40 at best glide speed. From 1000 m altitude, how far can it glide (still air)?
Glide distance = altitude × L/D
Distance = 1000 × 40
= 40,000 m = 40 km

Every 1 m of altitude trades for 40 m of forward distance. Modern sailplanes achieve L/D of 60+.

Answer: 40 km. L/D is the single most important efficiency metric in aviation. A Boeing 737 has L/D ≈ 17; a Cessna ≈ 8; an F-16 ≈ 9. Higher is better for fuel economy and glide range.

Bernoulli's Principle — Pressure, Velocity & the Origin of Lift

The foundational relationship between fluid speed and pressure — and why it's both powerful and commonly misunderstood.

1 Bernoulli's Equation

P + ½ρv² + ρgh = constant
P = static pressure (Pa)½ρv² = dynamic pressure (kinetic energy/vol)ρgh = hydrostatic pressure (for height changes)
Key insight: Along a streamline, as velocity increases, pressure MUST decrease (and vice versa). This is conservation of energy applied to flowing fluids. Valid for incompressible, inviscid flow along a streamline.
Worked Example 1
Pressure Drop Over a Wing
Problem: Air approaches a wing at 60 m/s. Over the upper surface, it accelerates to 75 m/s. What is the pressure difference? (ρ = 1.225 kg/m³, neglect height changes)
Bernoulli between two points
P₁ + ½ρv₁² = P₂ + ½ρv₂²
ΔP = P₁ − P₂ = ½ρ(v₂² − v₁²)
= ½ × 1.225 × (75² − 60²)
= 0.6125 × (5625 − 3600)
ΔP = 1,240 Pa (≈ 1.2% of atmospheric)
Answer: 1,240 Pa lower on the upper surface. Over a 16 m² wing, this creates 1240 × 16 = 19,840 N of lift — enough for a light aircraft. Even tiny pressure differences over large areas produce large forces.
Try the Bernoulli Calculator
Worked Example 2
Pitot Tube — Measuring Airspeed
Problem: A pitot tube on an aircraft reads total pressure of 103,500 Pa. Static pressure is 101,325 Pa. Air density is 1.225 kg/m³. What is the indicated airspeed?
Dynamic pressure → velocity
q = P_total − P_static = 103,500 − 101,325 = 2,175 Pa
q = ½ρv² → v = √(2q/ρ)
v = √(2 × 2175 / 1.225)
v = 59.6 m/s ≈ 116 knots
Answer: 59.6 m/s (116 knots). This is exactly how airspeed indicators work — they measure the pressure difference between a forward-facing tube (total pressure) and a side port (static pressure).

Reynolds Number — Laminar vs. Turbulent Flow

The dimensionless number that determines whether flow is smooth or chaotic — critical for all aerodynamic analysis.

1 Definition & Physical Meaning

Re = ρvL/μ = vL/ν
Re = inertial forces / viscous forcesν = μ/ρ = kinematic viscosity (m²/s)L = characteristic length (chord, diameter, etc.)
Laminar (Re < 5×10⁵)

Smooth, layered flow. Low skin friction but susceptible to separation. Think: honey flowing, or a model aircraft wing.

Turbulent (Re > 10⁶)

Chaotic, mixed flow. Higher friction but resists separation. Think: river rapids, or a full-size aircraft wing.

Boundary layer transition: Flow starts laminar near the leading edge and transitions to turbulent downstream. The turbulent boundary layer is thicker and has higher skin friction, but is more resistant to flow separation.
Worked Example 1
Reynolds Number for a Wing
Problem: A wing has a chord of 1.5 m and flies at 70 m/s at sea level (ρ = 1.225, μ = 1.81 × 10⁻⁵). Is the flow laminar or turbulent?
Calculate Re
Re = ρvL/μ = 1.225 × 70 × 1.5 / 1.81×10⁻⁵
Re = 7.1 × 10⁶ → Turbulent

Well above the transition threshold. Virtually all full-scale aircraft operate in turbulent flow regimes.

Answer: Re = 7.1 million (turbulent). Scale model testing must match Re for valid results — this is why wind tunnels use compressed air or special fluids.
Try the Reynolds Number Calculator
Worked Example 2
Scale Model Testing
Problem: You want to test a 1/10 scale model of the wing above in a wind tunnel at the same air conditions. What speed would give matching Reynolds number?
Match Re
Re_model = Re_full → ρv_m × L_m/μ = ρv_f × L_f/μ
v_m = v_f × (L_f/L_m) = 70 × 10
v_m = 700 m/s ≈ Mach 2!

Impractical — this is why wind tunnels use pressurized air (higher ρ) or accept Re mismatch and apply corrections.

Answer: 700 m/s — impossibly fast. This illustrates the "Reynolds number matching problem" in scale testing. Real solutions include cryogenic wind tunnels (dense cold air) or water tunnels (higher ρ and μ).

Mach Number & Compressible Flow — Supersonic & Shock Waves

When air speed approaches and exceeds the speed of sound — the physics changes dramatically.

1 Mach Number & Speed of Sound

M = v/a, where a = √(γRT)
γ = 1.4 (ratio of specific heats for air)R = 287 J/(kg·K), T in KelvinAt 15°C: a = 340.3 m/s = 1,225 km/h
M < 0.8
Subsonic (most prop aircraft)
0.8 < M < 1.2
Transonic (airliners: M ≈ 0.78–0.85)
1.2 < M < 5
Supersonic (fighter jets, Concorde: M 2.04)
M > 5
Hypersonic (re-entry vehicles, scramjets)
Worked Example 1
Mach Number at Altitude
Problem: A Boeing 787 cruises at 250 m/s at 11,000 m altitude where temperature is −56.5°C. What is its Mach number?
Speed of sound at altitude
T = −56.5 + 273.15 = 216.65 K
a = √(1.4 × 287 × 216.65) = √(87,066)
a = 295.1 m/s
M = 250 / 295.1
M = 0.847 (transonic)
Answer: Mach 0.847. The speed of sound drops from 340 m/s at sea level to 295 m/s at cruise altitude because it's colder. Airlines quote "Mach 0.85" rather than km/h because Mach accounts for this.
Try the Mach Number Calculator
Worked Example 2
Shock Wave Mach Cone Angle
Problem: A fighter jet flies at Mach 1.8. What is the half-angle of its Mach cone (shock wave angle)?
Mach cone formula
sin(μ) = 1/M = 1/1.8 = 0.5556
μ = arcsin(0.5556) = 33.7°

The Mach cone trailing behind the jet creates a sonic boom where it intersects the ground.

Answer: 33.7° half-angle. At Mach 1.0 the cone is 90° (flat — the shock is perpendicular). As speed increases, the cone narrows. At Mach 3, the angle is only 19.5°.

Airfoil Design — Shape, Stall & Performance

How wing cross-section geometry determines lift, drag, and stall characteristics.

1 Airfoil Geometry

Chord (c)
Leading edge to trailing edge
Camber
Curvature of the mean line (asymmetry)
Thickness (t/c)
Max thickness as % of chord (typically 12–18%)
Angle of attack (α)
Angle between chord line and freestream

2 Stall — The Lift Limit

V_stall = √(2W / ρSC_Lmax)
Stall occurs at critical angle of attack (~12–18°)Flow separates from upper surface → lift drops sharplyC_Lmax typically 1.2–1.6 (clean wing), up to 3.0 with flaps
Worked Example 1
Stall Speed Calculation
Problem: An aircraft weighs 15,000 N with wing area 20 m² and C_Lmax = 1.5. What is the stall speed at sea level?
Stall speed formula
V_s = √(2 × 15000 / (1.225 × 20 × 1.5))
= √(30000 / 36.75)
V_s = 28.6 m/s = 55.6 knots
Answer: 28.6 m/s (56 knots). With landing flaps (C_Lmax = 2.5), stall speed drops to 22.1 m/s (43 knots). This is why flaps are deployed for landing — they lower the stall speed.
Try the Stall Speed Calculator
Worked Example 2
Effect of Altitude on Stall Speed
Problem: The same aircraft flies at 6000 m altitude where ρ = 0.660 kg/m³. What is the new stall speed?
Same formula, different ρ
V_s = √(2 × 15000 / (0.660 × 20 × 1.5))
= √(30000 / 19.8)
V_s = 38.9 m/s = 75.6 knots (36% higher!)

Stall speed ratio: √(1.225/0.660) = 1.362 — stall speed increases as √(1/ρ).

Answer: 38.9 m/s (76 knots) — 36% higher than sea level. This is why aircraft must fly faster at altitude and why high-altitude airports have longer runways. Thin air = less lift at same speed.

Wind Engineering — Structural Loads, Buildings & Design

How wind creates forces on buildings, bridges, and structures — and how engineers design for safety.

1 Wind Pressure on Structures

F = ½ρv²C_DA
Same drag equation, applied to buildingsC_D for buildings ≈ 1.0–2.0Design codes use gust factors and exposure categories
v² means danger grows fast: Doubling wind speed from 50 km/h to 100 km/h quadruples the force. A Category 5 hurricane (250 km/h) exerts 25× more force than a 50 km/h breeze.

2 The Beaufort Scale (Selected)

Force 6: 39–49 km/h
Strong breeze — umbrellas difficult
Force 8: 62–74 km/h
Gale — twigs break, walking impeded
Force 10: 89–102 km/h
Storm — trees uprooted, structural damage
Force 12: >118 km/h
Hurricane — devastating damage
Worked Example 1
Wind Force on a Building Face
Problem: A building face is 15 m wide × 40 m tall. Wind speed is 130 km/h (36.1 m/s). C_D = 1.3, ρ = 1.225 kg/m³. What is the total wind force?
Calculate force
A = 15 × 40 = 600 m²
F = ½ × 1.225 × 36.1² × 1.3 × 600
= ½ × 1.225 × 1303.2 × 780
F = 623,000 N = 623 kN ≈ 63.5 tonnes force
Answer: 623 kN (63.5 tonnes) pushing on one face. This creates an enormous overturning moment at the base. The structural foundation must resist this — which is why skyscrapers have massive concrete cores.

Worked Example 2
Solar Panel Wind Loading
Problem: A rooftop solar array has 20 panels, each 1.7 m × 1.0 m, tilted at 30°. Design wind speed is 90 km/h (25 m/s). C_D for tilted flat plate ≈ 1.2. Will the mounting bolts (rated 500 N each, 4 per panel) hold?
Force per panel
Projected area = 1.7 × 1.0 × sin(30°) = 0.85 m²
F_panel = ½ × 1.225 × 25² × 1.2 × 0.85
= 0.6125 × 625 × 1.02
F_panel = 390 N per panel
Bolt capacity: 4 × 500 = 2000 N
Safety factor = 2000/390 = 5.1× ✓
Answer: 390 N per panel with safety factor 5.1× — the bolts hold. However, engineers apply gust factors (typically 1.5–2.5×), which reduces the effective margin. Always use local building codes for real designs.

Worked Example 3
Hurricane vs. Normal Wind Comparison
Problem: Compare the wind pressure at 50 km/h (normal), 120 km/h (strong storm), and 250 km/h (Cat 5 hurricane).
Dynamic pressure q = ½ρv²
50 km/h (13.9 m/s): q = ½ × 1.225 × 13.9² = 118 Pa
120 km/h (33.3 m/s): q = ½ × 1.225 × 33.3² = 680 Pa
250 km/h (69.4 m/s): q = ½ × 1.225 × 69.4² = 2,953 Pa
Ratio: 1× : 5.8× : 25×
Answer: Hurricane force is 25× stronger than a normal breeze. At 2,953 Pa over a 10 m² surface, that's 29,530 N (~3 tonnes). This is why hurricane building codes require impact-resistant windows and reinforced roofing.
