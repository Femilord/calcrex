Compound Interest ‚Äî The Most Powerful Force in Finance

How money grows when interest earns interest ‚Äî and why starting early matters more than investing more.

1 Simple vs. Compound Interest

Simple Interest
I = P √ó r √ó t

Interest only on original principal. Linear growth. Used in short-term loans.

Compound Interest
A = P √ó (1 + r/n)^(nt)

Interest on principal + accumulated interest. Exponential growth. Used in savings, investments.

A = final amountP = principal (initial investment)r = annual interest rate (decimal)n = compounding frequency per yeart = time in years
n = 1
Annually
n = 4
Quarterly
n = 12

Worked Example 1
Compound Interest ‚Äî Monthly Compounding
Problem: You invest $5,000 at 7% annual interest, compounded monthly, for 10 years. What is the final amount? How much is interest earned?
Step 1: Identify variables
P = $5,000, r = 0.07, n = 12, t = 10
Step 2: Apply formula
A = 5000 √ó (1 + 0.07/12)^(12 √ó 10)
= 5000 √ó (1.005833)^120
= 5000 √ó 2.00966
A = $10,048.31
Step 3: Interest earned
Interest = A ‚àí P = $10,048.31 ‚àí $5,000
Interest = $5,048.31
Answer: $10,048.31 total, with $5,048.31 in interest ‚Äî you've more than doubled your money. Simple interest would have yielded only $3,500 in interest ($8,500 total). That extra $1,548 is compound interest working on accumulated interest.

3 The Rule of 72

A quick mental math shortcut to estimate how long it takes to double your money.

Years to double ‚âà 72 / interest rate (%)
At 6%: 72/6 = 12 yearsAt 8%: 72/8 = 9 yearsAt 12%: 72/12 = 6 years
Works in reverse too: If prices double in 10 years, inflation ‚âà 72/10 = 7.2%. If your debt doubles in 6 years, you're paying ‚âà 72/6 = 12% interest.

Alice: 10 years of contributions, then 30 years of growth

Answer: Alice: $398,807 (invested $24K). Bob: $298,072 (invested $72K). Alice invested 3√ó less money but ends up with $100K more ‚Äî because her money had 30 extra years of compounding. Start early.
Worked Example 3
APR vs. APY ‚Äî What You Actually Earn
Problem: A savings account advertises 5% APR compounded daily. What is the actual APY (Annual Percentage Yield)?
Step 1: APY formula
APY = (1 + r/n)^n ‚àí 1

Answer: 5.127% APY. The more frequently interest compounds, the higher the effective yield. APR is the nominal rate; APY is what you actually earn. Always compare APY when choosing savings accounts.

Loans & Mortgages ‚Äî Monthly Payments, Amortization & Total Cost

How to calculate what you'll actually pay ‚Äî and strategies to save thousands in interest.

1 The Loan Payment Formula

The standard formula for fixed-rate loan payments (mortgages, car loans, personal loans).

M = P √ó [r(1+r)^n] / [(1+r)^n ‚àí 1]
M = monthly paymentP = principal (loan amount)r = monthly interest rate (annual rate / 12)n = total number of payments (years √ó 12)
Worked Example 1
Mortgage Payment Calculation
Problem: You take a $300,000 mortgage at 6.5% for 30 years. What is the monthly payment? How much total interest will you pay?
Step 1: Convert to monthly values
P = $300,000
r = 0.065/12 = 0.005417
n = 30 √ó 12 = 360 payments
Step 2: Apply formula
M = 300000 √ó [0.005417(1.005417)^360] / [(1.005417)^360 ‚àí 1]
= 300000 √ó [0.005417 √ó 6.9916] / [6.9916 ‚àí 1]
= 300000 √ó 0.03788 / 5.9916
M = $1,896.20/month
Step 3: Total cost & interest
Total paid = $1,896.20 √ó 360 = $682,632
Total interest = $682,632 ‚àí $300,000 = $382,632
Answer: $1,896.20/month, with $382,632 in total interest ‚Äî more than the original loan! This is why even small rate differences matter enormously over 30 years.

2 Amortization ‚Äî Where Your Payment Goes

Each payment is split between interest and principal. Early payments are mostly interest; later payments are mostly principal.

First payment breakdown on our $300K mortgage: Interest = $300,000 √ó 0.005417 = $1,625. Principal = $1,896.20 ‚àí $1,625 = $271.20. Only $271 reduces your balance! By payment #300, it flips: ~$1,700 goes to principal.
Worked Example 2
Extra Payments ‚Äî How Much Do You Save?
Problem: On the same $300,000 mortgage at 6.5%, you pay an extra $200/month. How many years do you save? How much interest?
Step 1: New payment amount
New M = $1,896.20 + $200 = $2,096.20
Step 2: Solve for new n
n = ‚àíln(1 ‚àí P√ór/M) / ln(1+r)
= ‚àíln(1 ‚àí 300000√ó0.005417/2096.20) / ln(1.005417)
= ‚àíln(0.2248) / 0.005402
n ‚âà 277 months = 23.1 years (saves ~7 years!)
Step 3: Interest saved
New total = $2,096.20 √ó 277 = $580,647
Old total = $682,632
Interest saved = $101,985
Answer: 7 years off the mortgage and ~$102,000 saved in interest ‚Äî by adding just $200/month. Extra payments go 100% to principal, which reduces future interest calculations.
Worked Example 3
Car Loan ‚Äî Total Cost Comparison
Problem: A $25,000 car loan at 4.9% for 5 years vs. 7.2% for 6 years. Compare monthly payments and total cost.
Option A: 4.9%, 5 years
r = 0.049/12 = 0.004083, n = 60
M = 25000 √ó 0.004083(1.004083)^60 / [(1.004083)^60 ‚àí 1]
M = $471.78/month | Total = $28,307
Option B: 7.2%, 6 years
r = 0.072/12 = 0.006, n = 72
M = 25000 √ó 0.006(1.006)^72 / [(1.006)^72 ‚àí 1]
M = $429.65/month | Total = $30,935
Answer: Option B has lower payments ($42 less/month) but costs $2,628 more overall. The "affordable" payment costs more. Always compare total cost, not just monthly payments.

Investments ‚Äî ROI, CAGR & Portfolio Returns

How to measure investment performance ‚Äî the formulas that separate real gains from misleading numbers.

1 Return on Investment (ROI)

ROI = (Gain ‚àí Cost) / Cost √ó 100%
Simple percentage returnDoes NOT account for time

2 CAGR ‚Äî Compound Annual Growth Rate

CAGR is the smoothed annual rate of return, accounting for compounding. It's the best way to compare investments of different durations.

CAGR = (Ending/Beginning)^(1/years) ‚àí 1
Annualized return ‚Äî allows apples-to-apples comparisonSmooths out volatility over the period
Worked Example 1
ROI vs. CAGR ‚Äî Which Is Better?
Problem: Investment A: $10,000 ‚Üí $18,000 in 5 years. Investment B: $10,000 ‚Üí $15,000 in 3 years. Which performed better?
Step 1: Simple ROI
ROI(A) = (18000 ‚àí 10000)/10000 = 80%
ROI(B) = (15000 ‚àí 10000)/10000 = 50%

ROI says A is better ‚Äî but that's misleading!

Step 2: CAGR (annualized)
CAGR(A) = (18000/10000)^(1/5) ‚àí 1 = 1.8^0.2 ‚àí 1
CAGR(A) = 12.47% per year
CAGR(B) = (15000/10000)^(1/3) ‚àí 1 = 1.5^0.333 ‚àí 1
CAGR(B) = 14.47% per year
Answer: B is actually better (14.47% vs 12.47% annually). If you reinvested B's returns for 5 years at the same rate, you'd have $19,687 ‚Äî more than A's $18,000. Always use CAGR to compare investments of different lengths.

3 Dollar-Cost Averaging (DCA)

Investing a fixed dollar amount at regular intervals, regardless of price. You buy more shares when prices are low and fewer when prices are high.

Why DCA works: Your average cost per share is always lower than the average price per share (harmonic mean < arithmetic mean). It also removes the emotional temptation to time the market.
Worked Example 2
Dollar-Cost Averaging in Action
Problem: You invest $500/month for 4 months. Share prices are: $50, $40, $25, $50. What is your average cost vs. average price?
Step 1: Shares purchased each month
Month 1: $500/$50 = 10 shares
Month 2: $500/$40 = 12.5 shares
Month 3: $500/$25 = 20 shares
Month 4: $500/$50 = 10 shares
Step 2: Compare averages
Total shares = 52.5
Total invested = $2,000
Avg cost/share = $2000/52.5 = $38.10
Avg price = ($50+$40+$25+$50)/4 = $41.25
Current value = 52.5 √ó $50 = $2,625
Gain = $625 (31.25% return)
Answer: Average cost $38.10 vs average price $41.25 ‚Äî DCA saved $3.15/share. Even though the price ended where it started ($50), you're up 31.25% because you bought heavily during the dip. DCA turns volatility into an advantage.
Worked Example 3
Real Return After Inflation
Problem: Your portfolio returned 9% this year. Inflation was 3.5%. What is your real (inflation-adjusted) return?
Step 1: Fisher equation (precise method)
Real return = [(1 + nominal)/(1 + inflation)] ‚àí 1
= (1.09/1.035) ‚àí 1
Real return = 5.31%
Quick approximation
Real ‚âà Nominal ‚àí Inflation = 9% ‚àí 3.5%
‚âà 5.5% (close but slightly overestimates)
Answer: 5.31% real return. Always adjust for inflation when evaluating long-term performance. A "12% return" during 8% inflation is only 3.7% real growth in purchasing power.

Time Value of Money ‚Äî Present Value, Future Value & Annuities

The central principle of all finance ‚Äî a dollar today is worth more than a dollar tomorrow.

1 Present Value & Future Value

Money has a time dimension. Present value (PV) answers "what is a future sum worth today?" Future value (FV) answers "what will today's money be worth later?"

Future Value
FV = PV √ó (1 + r)^n

Growing money forward. What will $X become?

Present Value
PV = FV / (1 + r)^n

Discounting money back. What is future $X worth now?

Worked Example 1
Present Value ‚Äî Is This Deal Worth It?
Problem: Someone offers you $50,000 five years from now, or $38,000 cash today. If you can earn 6% annually on investments, which is better?
Step 1: Find PV of the $50,000 offer
PV = FV / (1 + r)^n = 50000 / (1.06)^5
= 50000 / 1.3382
PV = $37,363
Step 2: Compare
$38,000 today > $37,363 (PV of future $50K)
Take the $38,000 cash today
Answer: Take $38,000 today. Invested at 6%, it grows to $50,853 in 5 years ‚Äî $853 more than the future offer. The discount rate (your alternative return) is what makes TVM decisions possible.

2 Annuities ‚Äî Streams of Equal Payments

An annuity is a series of equal payments at regular intervals ‚Äî like loan payments, pension income, or systematic investments.

FV(annuity) = PMT √ó [(1+r)^n ‚àí 1] / r
PMT = payment per periodr = interest rate per periodn = number of periods
PV(annuity) = PMT √ó [1 ‚àí (1+r)^(‚àín)] / r
Used to value loan streams, pensions, leases
Worked Example 2
Annuity ‚Äî Saving for a Goal
Problem: You want $100,000 in 15 years. You can earn 7% annually, compounded monthly. How much must you save each month?
Step 1: Rearrange FV annuity for PMT
PMT = FV √ó r / [(1+r)^n ‚àí 1]
r = 0.07/12 = 0.005833, n = 180
Step 2: Calculate
PMT = 100000 √ó 0.005833 / [(1.005833)^180 ‚àí 1]
= 583.33 / [2.8484 ‚àí 1]
= 583.33 / 1.8484
PMT = $315.58/month
Answer: $315.58/month. Total contributions: $56,804. Interest earned: $43,196. Almost half the goal comes from compound returns ‚Äî the longer your timeline, the more compounding does the heavy lifting.
Worked Example 3
PV of Annuity ‚Äî What's a Pension Worth?
Problem: A pension pays $2,000/month for 25 years. If the discount rate is 5%, what is the lump-sum present value?
Step 1: Monthly rate and periods
r = 0.05/12 = 0.004167, n = 300
Step 2: PV annuity formula
PV = 2000 √ó [1 ‚àí (1.004167)^(‚àí300)] / 0.004167
= 2000 √ó [1 ‚àí 0.2873] / 0.004167
= 2000 √ó 170.95
PV = $341,902
Answer: $341,902. The pension will pay $600,000 total, but discounted to today it's worth $342K. This is how insurance companies price annuity buyouts and how courts value future income in lawsuits.

Business Finance ‚Äî Break-Even, Profit Margin & Cash Flow

The numbers every business owner and finance student needs ‚Äî from pricing decisions to profitability analysis.

1 Break-Even Analysis

The break-even point is where total revenue equals total costs ‚Äî no profit, no loss. Every unit sold beyond this point generates profit.

BEP (units) = Fixed Costs / (Price ‚àí Variable Cost)
Price ‚àí Variable Cost = Contribution Margin per unitBEP (revenue) = Fixed Costs / CM RatioCM Ratio = Contribution Margin / Price
Worked Example 1
Break-Even Point
Problem: A bakery has fixed costs of $4,500/month. Each cake costs $8 to make and sells for $25. How many cakes must they sell to break even? What revenue is needed?
Step 1: Contribution margin
CM = Price ‚àí Variable Cost = $25 ‚àí $8
CM = $17 per cake
Step 2: Break-even units
BEP = $4,500 / $17
BEP = 265 cakes/month
Step 3: Break-even revenue
Revenue = 265 √ó $25
= $6,625/month
Answer: 265 cakes ($6,625 revenue). Every cake after #265 generates $17 profit. If they sell 350 cakes: profit = (350 ‚àí 265) √ó $17 = $1,445/month.

2 Profit Margins

Gross Margin
(Revenue ‚àí COGS) / Revenue √ó 100
Operating Margin
(Revenue ‚àí COGS ‚àí OpEx) / Revenue √ó 100
Net Margin
Net Income / Revenue √ó 100
Markup
(Price ‚àí Cost) / Cost √ó 100
Margin ‚â† Markup! A product bought for $60 and sold for $100 has 40% margin but 66.7% markup. Margin is based on selling price; markup is based on cost. Confusing them is a costly mistake in pricing.
Worked Example 2
Margin vs. Markup
Problem: A retailer wants a 40% profit margin on a product that costs $45 wholesale. What should the selling price be? What is the markup percentage?
Step 1: Selling price from margin
Margin = (Price ‚àí Cost)/Price
0.40 = (Price ‚àí 45)/Price
Price ‚àí 0.40 √ó Price = 45
0.60 √ó Price = 45
Price = $75.00
Step 2: Markup percentage
Markup = (75 ‚àí 45)/45 √ó 100
Markup = 66.7%
Answer: Sell at $75 for 40% margin (66.7% markup). Quick conversion: Margin = Markup/(1+Markup). Markup = Margin/(1‚àíMargin). A 50% margin = 100% markup (doubling the cost).
Worked Example 3
Net Present Value (NPV) ‚Äî Should You Take the Project?
Problem: A project costs $50,000 upfront and generates $15,000/year for 5 years. Required return is 10%. Should you invest?
Step 1: PV of each year's cash flow
Year 1: $15,000/1.10 = $13,636
Year 2: $15,000/1.21 = $12,397
Year 3: $15,000/1.331 = $11,270
Year 4: $15,000/1.4641 = $10,245
Year 5: $15,000/1.6105 = $9,314
Step 2: Calculate NPV
Sum of PVs = $56,862
NPV = $56,862 ‚àí $50,000
NPV = +$6,862 ‚Üí ACCEPT the project
Answer: NPV = +$6,862. Positive NPV means the project returns more than the 10% required rate. The total undiscounted cash is $75,000, but adjusted for time and risk, the net gain is $6,862 in today's dollars.

Retirement Planning ‚Äî Savings Goals, Withdrawal Rates & Inflation

How much do you actually need to retire? The math behind the 4% rule, required savings, and making your money last.

1 The 4% Rule (Safe Withdrawal Rate)

The 4% rule suggests you can withdraw 4% of your portfolio in year 1, then adjust for inflation each year, and your money should last 30+ years.

Retirement Nest Egg = Annual Expenses √ó 25
Based on 4% withdrawal rate (1/0.04 = 25)Need $40K/year ‚Üí save $1,000,000Need $60K/year ‚Üí save $1,500,000
Why 25√ó? The Trinity Study showed that a 4% initial withdrawal rate, adjusted for inflation, survived 30-year periods 95% of the time with a 50/50 stock/bond portfolio. It's a starting guideline, not a guarantee.
Worked Example 1
How Much Do You Need to Retire?
Problem: You spend $4,500/month now and expect to need 80% of that in retirement. Inflation averages 3%. You retire in 20 years. What nest egg do you need (in future dollars)?
Step 1: Current retirement spending need
Annual need = $4,500 √ó 12 √ó 0.80 = $43,200
Step 2: Adjust for inflation (20 years)
Future annual need = $43,200 √ó (1.03)^20
= $43,200 √ó 1.8061
= $78,024/year in future dollars
Step 3: Apply 25√ó rule
Nest egg = $78,024 √ó 25 = $1,950,600
Answer: ~$1.95 million. Without adjusting for inflation, you'd target only $1.08M ‚Äî and run out of money because $43,200 won't buy the same goods in 20 years. Always plan in future dollars.

2 Required Monthly Savings

Once you know your target nest egg, work backwards to find the monthly savings needed using the FV annuity formula.

PMT = FV √ó r / [(1+r)^n ‚àí 1]
FV = retirement targetr = monthly return raten = months until retirement
Worked Example 2
Monthly Savings to Reach Your Goal
Problem: You need $1,950,600 in 20 years. You already have $50,000 saved. Expected return: 8% annually. How much must you save monthly?
Step 1: Future value of existing savings
FV(existing) = $50,000 √ó (1 + 0.08/12)^240
= $50,000 √ó 4.9268 = $246,341
Step 2: Remaining gap
Gap = $1,950,600 ‚àí $246,341 = $1,704,259
Step 3: Monthly savings needed
r = 0.08/12 = 0.00667, n = 240
PMT = 1,704,259 √ó 0.00667 / [(1.00667)^240 ‚àí 1]
= 11,364 / [4.9268 ‚àí 1]
PMT = $2,893/month
Answer: $2,893/month. Total contributions: $50,000 + ($2,893 √ó 240) = $744,320. The remaining $1.2M comes from investment returns ‚Äî compounding does most of the work over 20 years.
Worked Example 3
How Long Will Your Savings Last?
Problem: You retire with $800,000 and withdraw $3,500/month. Your portfolio earns 5% annually. How many years will it last?
Step 1: Monthly rate
r = 0.05/12 = 0.004167
Step 2: Solve for n (months)
n = ‚àíln(1 ‚àí PV√ór/PMT) / ln(1+r)
= ‚àíln(1 ‚àí 800000√ó0.004167/3500) / ln(1.004167)
= ‚àíln(1 ‚àí 0.9524) / 0.004158
= ‚àíln(0.0476) / 0.004158
= 3.045 / 0.004158
n ‚âà 732 months ‚âà 61 years
Answer: ~61 years ‚Äî well beyond a typical retirement. The 5% return almost covers the withdrawals ($3,333/month in interest vs $3,500 withdrawn), so the principal depletes very slowly. If returns drop to 3%, it lasts only 28 years ‚Äî highlighting why return assumptions matter enormously.
