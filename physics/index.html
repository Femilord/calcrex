Explore the most commonly studied physics concepts with clear explanations, diagrams, step-by-step solved problems, and real-world applications.

Newton's Laws of Motion & Applications

Understanding the fundamental laws that govern all motion — from falling apples to orbiting planets.

1 The Three Laws of Motion

First Law — Law of Inertia

An object at rest stays at rest, and an object in motion stays in motion with the same speed and direction, unless acted upon by an unbalanced external force.

Inertia is the tendency of an object to resist changes in its state of motion. Mass is the quantitative measure of inertia — the greater the mass, the greater the resistance to acceleration.
Second Law — F = ma

The acceleration of an object is directly proportional to the net force acting on it and inversely proportional to its mass.

Fnet = m × a
Fnet = Net force (N)m = Mass (kg)a = Acceleration (m/s²)

This is the most widely used law in physics. The direction of acceleration is always in the direction of the net force.

Third Law — Action & Reaction

For every action, there is an equal and opposite reaction. When object A exerts a force on object B, object B simultaneously exerts an equal force in the opposite direction on object A.

The action-reaction pair always acts on different objects. This is why they don't cancel out.

2 Free Body Diagrams (FBDs)

A free body diagram isolates a single object and shows all forces acting on it as vectors. This is the essential first step in solving any force problem.

Figure 1: Free body diagram of a block on a horizontal surface. Weight (W = mg) acts downward, normal force (N) acts upward, friction (f = μN) opposes motion, and applied force pushes the block right.
Steps to Draw a Free Body Diagram
1Identify the object and draw it as a simple shape (box or dot).
2Identify every force acting on the object (not forces it exerts).
3Draw each force as an arrow pointing in the correct direction.
4Label all forces and choose a coordinate system.

3 Equilibrium — When Forces Balance

An object is in static equilibrium when at rest with zero net force. Dynamic equilibrium means constant velocity with zero net force.

ΣF = 0
ΣFx = 0 (horizontal forces balance)ΣFy = 0 (vertical forces balance)
Worked Example 1
Block on a Horizontal Surface with Friction
Problem: A 15 kg box is pushed along a horizontal floor with a force of 80 N. The coefficient of kinetic friction is μk = 0.30. Find (a) the normal force, (b) friction force, and (c) acceleration.
Step 1: Known values

m = 15 kg, Fapp = 80 N, μk = 0.30, g = 9.81 m/s²

Step 2: Vertical equilibrium (ΣFy = 0)
N − mg = 0
N = mg = 15 × 9.81
N = 147.15 N
Step 3: Friction force
fk = μk × N = 0.30 × 147.15
fk = 44.15 N
Step 4: Horizontal — ΣFx = ma
Fapp − fk = ma
80 − 44.15 = 15a
a = 2.39 m/s²
Answers: (a) N = 147.15 N, (b) fk = 44.15 N, (c) a = 2.39 m/s² to the right.
Worked Example 2
Block on an Inclined Plane
Problem: A 10 kg block slides down a frictionless 30° incline. Find the acceleration and normal force.
Figure 2: On an incline, gravity decomposes into mg sin θ (along surface, causes sliding) and mg cos θ (perpendicular, balanced by normal force).
Step 1: Decompose gravity
Along incline: mg sin 30° = 10 × 9.81 × 0.5 = 49.05 N
Perpendicular: mg cos 30° = 10 × 9.81 × 0.866 = 84.96 N
Step 2: Normal force (ΣF⊥ = 0)
N = mg cos 30° = 84.96 N
Step 3: Acceleration along incline
mg sin θ = ma → a = g sin 30°
a = 4.905 m/s²
Answers: a = 4.905 m/s² down the incline, N = 84.96 N. The acceleration depends only on angle and g — not mass!
Worked Example 3
Atwood Machine — Two Connected Masses
Problem: Two masses m₁ = 5 kg and m₂ = 3 kg are connected by a string over a frictionless pulley. Find the acceleration and string tension.
Figure 3: Atwood Machine — heavier mass accelerates down, lighter mass accelerates up with same magnitude.
Step 1: Newton's Second Law for each mass
m₁: m₁g − T = m₁a ... (1)
m₂: T − m₂g = m₂a ... (2)
Step 2: Add equations to find acceleration
a = (m₁ − m₂)g / (m₁ + m₂) = (5−3)(9.81) / (5+3)
a = 2.45 m/s²
Step 3: Find tension
T = m₂(g + a) = 3(9.81 + 2.45)
T = 36.79 N
Answers: a = 2.45 m/s², T = 36.79 N. The tension lies between m₁g (49.05 N) and m₂g (29.43 N).

Kinematics — Projectile Motion, Free Fall & Acceleration

Describing motion without worrying about forces — the mathematics of movement.

1 The Kinematic Equations (SUVAT)

These four equations relate displacement (s), initial velocity (u), final velocity (v), acceleration (a), and time (t) for constant acceleration.

v = u + at
Missing: s
s = ut + ½at²
Missing: v
v² = u² + 2as
Missing: t
s = ½(u + v)t
Missing: a
Tip: To pick the right equation, identify which variable is not given and not asked for — then choose the equation that doesn't contain it.

2 Free Fall

Free fall is motion under gravity alone with a = g = 9.81 m/s² (downward). Air resistance is neglected. All objects fall at the same rate regardless of mass.

Worked Example 1
Object Dropped from a Height
Problem: A ball is dropped from rest from a 45 m building. Find (a) the time to reach the ground, and (b) impact velocity.
Step 1: Known values (downward positive)
u = 0, a = 9.81 m/s², s = 45 m
Step 2: Time — s = ut + ½at²
45 = 0 + ½(9.81)t²
t² = 45/4.905 = 9.174
t = 3.03 s
Step 3: Velocity — v = u + at
v = 0 + 9.81 × 3.03
v = 29.72 m/s ≈ 107 km/h
Answers: (a) t = 3.03 s, (b) v = 29.72 m/s downward.

3 Projectile Motion

A projectile moves under gravity only after launch. The key insight: horizontal and vertical motions are independent.

Horizontal (x)

No acceleration (ax = 0)

vx = v₀ cos θ = constant
x = v₀ cos(θ) × t
Vertical (y)

Acceleration = −g (upward positive)

vy = v₀ sin θ − gt
y = v₀ sin(θ)t − ½gt²
Figure 4: Projectile trajectory (parabola). At the peak, vy = 0. Maximum range at θ = 45°.
Key Projectile Formulas
Max Height: H = v₀² sin²θ / (2g) Time of Flight: T = 2v₀ sin θ / g Range: R = v₀² sin(2θ) / g
Worked Example 2
Projectile Launched at an Angle
Problem: A football is kicked at 25 m/s at 40° above horizontal. Find (a) max height, (b) time of flight, (c) range.
Step 1: Resolve velocity
v₀ₓ = 25 cos 40° = 19.15 m/s
v₀ᵧ = 25 sin 40° = 16.07 m/s
Step 2: Maximum height
H = v₀²sin²θ / (2g) = 625 × 0.413 / 19.62
H = 13.16 m
Step 3: Time of flight
T = 2v₀ sin θ / g = 2(16.07)/9.81
T = 3.28 s
Step 4: Range
R = v₀²sin(2θ)/g = 625 × sin 80° / 9.81
R = 62.73 m
Answers: (a) H = 13.16 m, (b) T = 3.28 s, (c) R = 62.73 m.
Worked Example 3
Car Braking — Stopping Distance
Problem: A car at 90 km/h (25 m/s) brakes at 8 m/s². Find (a) stopping time and (b) stopping distance.
Step 1: Known values
u = 25 m/s, v = 0, a = −8 m/s²
Step 2: Time — v = u + at
0 = 25 − 8t → t = 25/8
t = 3.125 s
Step 3: Distance — v² = u² + 2as
0 = 625 − 16s
s = 39.06 m
Answers: (a) t = 3.125 s, (b) s = 39.06 m. Doubling speed quadruples stopping distance (s ∝ u²).

Energy & Momentum Conservation — Collisions

Two of the most powerful conservation laws in physics — energy and momentum are never lost, only transformed.

1 Conservation of Energy

Energy cannot be created or destroyed — only transformed. Total mechanical energy (kinetic + potential) stays constant when only conservative forces act.

KE₁ + PE₁ = KE₂ + PE₂
½mv₁² + mgh₁ = ½mv₂² + mgh₂
With friction: KE₁ + PE₁ = KE₂ + PE₂ + Wfriction (some energy converts to heat).
Worked Example 1
Roller Coaster — Energy Conservation
Problem: A 500 kg roller coaster starts from rest at the top of a 40 m hill. Find its speed at the bottom and at a second 25 m hill. (No friction.)
Figure 5: Energy conservation on a roller coaster — total mechanical energy stays constant throughout.
Step 1: Speed at bottom (h = 0)
mgh₁ = ½mv² → v = √(2gh₁) = √(2 × 9.81 × 40)
v = 28.01 m/s ≈ 100.8 km/h
Step 2: Speed at second hill (h₂ = 25 m)
v₂ = √(2g(h₁ − h₂)) = √(2 × 9.81 × 15)
v₂ = 17.15 m/s ≈ 61.7 km/h
Answers: Bottom speed = 28.01 m/s. Second hill speed = 17.15 m/s. Mass cancels — heavier cars reach the same speed!

2 Conservation of Momentum

Total momentum of an isolated system remains constant. Momentum is always conserved in collisions.

m₁v₁ + m₂v₂ = m₁v₁' + m₂v₂'
Total momentum before = Total momentum after

Worked Example 2
Elastic Collision — Equal Mass Billiard Balls
Problem: A 0.17 kg billiard ball at 5 m/s strikes an identical stationary ball head-on (elastic). Find both velocities after collision.
Step 1: Conservation of momentum
m₁v₁ + m₂v₂ = m₁v₁' + m₂v₂'
0.17(5) + 0 = 0.17v₁' + 0.17v₂'
5 = v₁' + v₂' ... (1)
Step 2: Conservation of kinetic energy
½m₁v₁² = ½m₁v₁'² + ½m₂v₂'²
25 = v₁'² + v₂'² ... (2)
Step 3: Equal-mass shortcut — velocities exchange
v₁' = 0 m/s (first ball stops)
v₂' = 5 m/s (second ball moves at original speed)

Verification: momentum = 0.85 kg⋅m/s before and after ✓. KE = 2.125 J before and after ✓.

Answer: Equal-mass elastic collision: moving ball stops, stationary ball takes its velocity. This is the "Newton's Cradle" effect.
Worked Example 3
Perfectly Inelastic — Bullet in Block
Problem: A 0.01 kg bullet at 400 m/s embeds in a 2 kg block at rest. Find (a) combined velocity, (b) kinetic energy lost.
Step 1: Conservation of momentum (stick together)
m₁v₁ = (m₁ + m₂)v'
0.01(400) = (2.01)v'
v' = 1.99 m/s
Step 2: Energy lost
KEbefore = ½(0.01)(400²) = 800 J
KEafter = ½(2.01)(1.99²) = 3.98 J
Energy lost = 796.02 J (99.5%!)
Answers: (a) v' = 1.99 m/s. (b) 796 J lost to heat, sound, deformation. Perfectly inelastic collisions lose the most kinetic energy.

Waves — Interference, Diffraction & Doppler Effect

How energy propagates through space without the transfer of matter.

1 Wave Fundamentals

v = fλ
v = wave speed (m/s), f = frequency (Hz), λ = wavelength (m)
Figure 6: Transverse wave. Wavelength (λ) = distance between two consecutive crests. Amplitude (A) = maximum displacement from equilibrium.

Transverse waves oscillate perpendicular to propagation (light, water surface). Longitudinal waves oscillate parallel to propagation (sound). Sound in air travels at approximately 343 m/s at 20°C.

2 Interference

When two waves overlap, the principle of superposition applies: the resultant displacement equals the sum of individual displacements.

Constructive Interference

Waves in phase — crests align with crests. Path difference = nλ (n = 0, 1, 2...). Amplitudes add → louder/brighter.

Destructive Interference

Waves out of phase — crests align with troughs. Path difference = (n + ½)λ. Amplitudes cancel → silence/darkness.

Young's Double-Slit Experiment
Bright fringes: d sin θ = nλ (n = 0, ±1, ±2...) Dark fringes: d sin θ = (n + ½)λ Fringe spacing: Δy = λL / d

3 Diffraction

Diffraction is the bending and spreading of waves around obstacles or through openings. Most noticeable when the opening size ≈ wavelength. A single slit of width a gives minima at:

a sin θ = nλ (n = ±1, ±2, ...)
a = slit width, θ = angle to minimum, n = order (≠ 0)
Wide slit → narrow central maximum (less spreading). Narrow slit → wide central maximum (more spreading). The central maximum is always twice the width of the other maxima.

4 The Doppler Effect

The apparent change in frequency when source and observer are in relative motion. You hear higher pitch as an ambulance approaches, lower as it recedes.

f' = f × (v ± vo) / (v ∓ vs)
f' = observed frequency, f = source frequencyv = speed of sound, vo = observer speed, vs = source speed
Sign convention: Top signs (+vo, −vs) when moving toward each other (frequency ↑). Bottom signs when moving apart (frequency ↓).
Worked Example 1
Doppler Effect — Approaching Ambulance
Problem: Ambulance siren: 700 Hz. Speed: 30 m/s toward stationary observer. Speed of sound: 343 m/s. Find heard frequency (a) approaching, (b) receding.
Step 1: Approaching
f' = f × v / (v − vs) = 700 × 343 / (343 − 30)
f' = 700 × 343 / 313
f' = 767.1 Hz (higher pitch)
Step 2: Receding
f' = f × v / (v + vs) = 700 × 343 / (343 + 30)
f' = 700 × 343 / 373
f' = 643.7 Hz (lower pitch)
Answers: Approaching: 767.1 Hz. Receding: 643.7 Hz. The shift is 123.4 Hz — very noticeable.
Worked Example 2
Young's Double-Slit — Fringe Spacing
Problem: Light of wavelength 550 nm passes through slits separated by 0.25 mm. Screen is 2 m away. Find (a) fringe spacing, and (b) angle to second bright fringe.
Step 1: Fringe spacing
Δy = λL / d = (550 × 10⁻⁹ × 2) / (0.25 × 10⁻³)
Δy = 4.4 × 10⁻³ m = 4.4 mm
Step 2: Angle to 2nd-order bright fringe (n = 2)
d sin θ = nλ
sin θ = nλ/d = 2(550 × 10⁻⁹) / (0.25 × 10⁻³)
sin θ = 0.0044
θ = 0.252° ≈ 0.25°
Answers: (a) Fringe spacing = 4.4 mm. (b) θ₂ = 0.25°. The small angle confirms the fringes are closely spaced — this is why you need a screen far from the slits to observe them clearly.

Electricity — Ohm's Law, Circuits & Electromagnetic Induction

Understanding current flow, circuit analysis, and how changing magnetic fields create electricity.

1 Ohm's Law & Basic Relationships

Ohm's Law relates voltage, current, and resistance in a conductor.

V = I × R
V = Voltage (V), I = Current (A), R = Resistance (Ω)

Power formulas: P = VI = I²R = V²/R

2 Series and Parallel Circuits

Series Circuit

Components end-to-end. Same current through all.

Rtotal = R₁ + R₂ + R₃
Vtotal = V₁ + V₂ + V₃
Itotal = I₁ = I₂ = I₃
Parallel Circuit

Components across same two points. Same voltage.

1/RT = 1/R₁ + 1/R₂ + 1/R₃
Itotal = I₁ + I₂ + I₃
Vtotal = V₁ = V₂ = V₃
Series Circuit
+──[ R₁ ]──[ R₂ ]──[ R₃ ]──+ | | [V] Same current I [V] | | +────────────────────────────+
Parallel Circuit
+──[ R₁ ]──+ | | +────+──[ R₂ ]──+────+ | | | | [V] +──[ R₃ ]──+ [V] | | +─────────────────────+
Parallel total resistance is always less than the smallest individual resistor.
Worked Example 1
Series-Parallel Combination Circuit
Problem: A 12 V battery connects to R₁ = 4 Ω in series with R₂ = 6 Ω ∥ R₃ = 3 Ω. Find total resistance, current, voltage drops, and branch currents.
Step 1: Parallel combination
1/Rp = 1/6 + 1/3 = 3/6
Rparallel = 2 Ω
Step 2: Total resistance
Rtotal = R₁ + Rp = 4 + 2 = 6 Ω
Step 3: Total current
I = V/R = 12/6 = 2 A
Step 4: Voltage drops
VR₁ = 2 × 4 = 8 V
Vparallel = 2 × 2 = 4 V

Check: 8 + 4 = 12 V ✓ (KVL)

Step 5: Branch currents
IR₂ = 4/6 = 0.667 A
IR₃ = 4/3 = 1.333 A

Check: 0.667 + 1.333 = 2 A ✓ (KCL)

Answers: Rtotal = 6 Ω, I = 2 A, VR₁ = 8 V, Vparallel = 4 V, IR₂ = 0.667 A, IR₃ = 1.333 A.

3 Kirchhoff's Laws

KCL — Junction Rule

Total current entering a junction equals total current leaving. (Conservation of charge.)

ΣIin = ΣIout
KVL — Loop Rule

Sum of voltage gains and drops around any closed loop = 0. (Conservation of energy.)

ΣV = 0 (around any closed loop)

4 Electromagnetic Induction

A changing magnetic flux through a circuit induces an EMF. This powers generators, transformers, and wireless charging.

Faraday's Law: ε = −N × ΔΦ/Δt
ε = induced EMF (V), N = number of turnsΔΦ/Δt = rate of change of magnetic flux (Wb/s)
Lenz's Law: The induced current flows in a direction that opposes the change in flux that caused it. This is the reason for the negative sign in Faraday's Law and is a consequence of energy conservation.
Worked Example 2
Electromagnetic Induction — Generator Coil
Problem: A 200-turn coil has an area of 0.05 m². The magnetic field through it changes from 0.3 T to 0 T in 0.02 s. Find the induced EMF.
Step 1: Calculate change in flux
ΔΦ = ΔB × A = (0 − 0.3) × 0.05 = −0.015 Wb
Step 2: Apply Faraday's Law
ε = −N × ΔΦ/Δt = −200 × (−0.015) / 0.02
ε = 150 V
Answer: The induced EMF is 150 V. The faster the field changes or the more turns in the coil, the greater the induced voltage.
Worked Example 3
Electrical Power & Energy Cost
Problem: A 2000 W electric heater runs for 3 hours. If electricity costs $0.12/kWh, find (a) the energy consumed and (b) the cost.
Step 1: Energy in kWh
E = P × t = 2 kW × 3 h
E = 6 kWh
Step 2: Cost
Cost = E × rate = 6 × $0.12
Cost = $0.72
Answers: (a) 6 kWh = 21.6 MJ. (b) Cost = $0.72. In Joules: E = 2000 × 10800 = 21,600,000 J.

Thermodynamics — Heat Transfer & Ideal Gas Law

The science of heat, energy transfer, and the behavior of gases — from steam engines to refrigerators.

1 Heat Transfer Methods

Conduction

Heat transfer through direct contact between molecules. Metals are good conductors; wood and air are insulators.

Q/t = kA(T₂ − T₁)/L
Convection & Radiation

Convection: Heat transfer by fluid movement (hot air rises). Radiation: Heat transfer via electromagnetic waves (no medium needed — how the Sun heats Earth).

2 Specific Heat & Calorimetry

The amount of heat needed to change the temperature of a substance depends on its mass, specific heat capacity, and temperature change.

Q = mcΔT
Q = heat energy (J)m = mass (kg), c = specific heat capacity (J/kg⋅K)ΔT = temperature change (K or °C)
Water has one of the highest specific heats: c = 4186 J/(kg⋅K). This is why oceans moderate coastal climates and why water is used in cooling systems.
Water: c = 4186 J/(kg⋅K)
Liquid
Ice: c = 2090 J/(kg⋅K)
Solid
Aluminum: c = 897 J/(kg⋅K)
Metal
Iron: c = 449 J/(kg⋅K)
Metal
Copper: c = 385 J/(kg⋅K)
Metal
Worked Example 1
Heating Water — How Much Energy?
Problem: How much energy is needed to heat 2 kg of water from 20°C to 100°C? If a 1500 W kettle does this, how long does it take?
Step 1: Calculate heat energy
Q = mcΔT = 2 × 4186 × (100 − 20)
Q = 2 × 4186 × 80
Q = 669,760 J ≈ 670 kJ
Step 2: Time for kettle (P = E/t → t = E/P)
t = Q / P = 669,760 / 1500
t = 446.5 s ≈ 7.4 minutes
Answers: Q = 670 kJ. Time ≈ 7.4 minutes. In practice it takes longer due to heat losses to the surroundings.
Worked Example 2
Calorimetry — Mixing Hot and Cold Water
Problem: 0.5 kg of water at 80°C is mixed with 1.5 kg of water at 20°C in an insulated container. Find the final equilibrium temperature.
Step 1: Heat lost = Heat gained (energy conservation)
mhotc(Thot − Tf) = mcoldc(Tf − Tcold)

Since both are water, c cancels:

0.5(80 − Tf) = 1.5(Tf − 20)
Step 2: Solve for Tf
40 − 0.5Tf = 1.5Tf − 30
70 = 2Tf
Tf = 35°C
Answer: Final temperature = 35°C. The result is closer to the cold water temperature because there is more cold water (larger mass dominates).

3 Ideal Gas Law

The ideal gas law relates pressure, volume, temperature, and amount of gas. It works well for most gases at moderate temperatures and pressures.

PV = nRT
P = pressure (Pa), V = volume (m³)n = moles, R = 8.314 J/(mol⋅K)T = temperature (Kelvin! Always!)
Always use Kelvin! T(K) = T(°C) + 273.15. The gas laws break down if you use Celsius or Fahrenheit.

For a fixed amount of gas changing between two states:

P₁V₁ / T₁ = P₂V₂ / T₂
This is the Combined Gas Law — useful when n is constant
Boyle's Law: P₁V₁ = P₂V₂
Constant T
Charles's Law: V₁/T₁ = V₂/T₂
Constant P
Gay-Lussac's: P₁/T₁ = P₂/T₂
Constant V
Worked Example 3
Ideal Gas Law — Finding Pressure
Problem: 2 moles of an ideal gas occupy 0.05 m³ at 300 K. Find the pressure. Then the gas is heated to 600 K at constant volume — find the new pressure.
Step 1: Find initial pressure using PV = nRT
P = nRT / V = (2 × 8.314 × 300) / 0.05
P₁ = 99,768 Pa ≈ 99.8 kPa ≈ 1 atm
Step 2: Heated at constant volume (Gay-Lussac's Law)
P₁/T₁ = P₂/T₂
P₂ = P₁ × T₂/T₁ = 99,768 × 600/300
P₂ = 199,536 Pa ≈ 199.5 kPa ≈ 2 atm
Answers: P₁ ≈ 99.8 kPa (≈ 1 atm). P₂ ≈ 199.5 kPa (≈ 2 atm). Doubling temperature (in Kelvin) at constant volume doubles the pressure — which is why aerosol cans warn against heating.

4 Laws of Thermodynamics

First Law — Energy Conservation

The change in internal energy of a system equals the heat added minus the work done by the system.

ΔU = Q − W
ΔU = change in internal energy, Q = heat added, W = work done by system
Second Law — Entropy Always Increases

Heat flows spontaneously from hot to cold, never the reverse. The total entropy of an isolated system always increases. No heat engine can be 100% efficient.

Efficiency: η = W/QH = 1 − QC/QH
Carnot (maximum): ηmax = 1 − TC/TH
Worked Example 4
Carnot Efficiency — Heat Engine
Problem: A heat engine operates between a hot reservoir at 500°C and a cold reservoir at 25°C. Find the maximum possible (Carnot) efficiency. If the engine absorbs 1000 J of heat, how much work can it produce?
Step 1: Convert to Kelvin
TH = 500 + 273 = 773 K
TC = 25 + 273 = 298 K
Step 2: Carnot efficiency
ηmax = 1 − TC/TH = 1 − 298/773
ηmax = 0.614 = 61.4%
Step 3: Maximum work output
W = η × QH = 0.614 × 1000
W = 614 J
Answers: Maximum efficiency = 61.4%. Max work = 614 J. The remaining 386 J must be rejected to the cold reservoir. Real engines always have lower efficiency than Carnot.
